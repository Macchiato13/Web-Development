REDOX DEFINITION

An Oxidation-Reduction reaction, commonly referred to as a Redox reaction, occurs when the oxidation number of a molecule, atom, or ion changes due to the gain or loss of an electron. These processes typically entail the transfer of oxygen atoms, hydrogen atoms, or electrons. There are Two Fundamental characteristics define redox reactions:

Firstly, they are coupled, meaning that for every oxidation reaction, there is a corresponding reduction reaction.

Secondly, they involve a distinct net chemical change, where an atom or electron transitions from one unit of matter to another.

Redox reactions are all around us: the burning of fuels, the corrosion of metals, and even the processes of photosynthesis and cellular respiration involve oxidation and reduction.

The ion or molecule that accepts electrons is called the oxidizing agent - by accepting electrons, it oxidizes other species.


The ion or molecule that donates electrons is called the reducing agent - by giving electrons, it reduces the other species.

OXIDATION STATES AND NUMBERS

In some cases, it is possible to tell by visual inspection. For example, we could have determined that the rusting of iron is a redox process by simply noting that it involves the formation of ions Fe3+ and O2 from free elements Fe and O2. But in most cases, the reaction is not as obvious, particularly when the reaction involves nonmetal elements.


To help identify these less obvious redox reactions, there is a concept of oxidation numbers, which provides a way to track electrons before and after a reaction. An atoms oxidation number (or oxidation state) is the imaginary charge that the atom would have if all of the bonds to the atom were completely ionic.


For example of the element vanadium, which forms a number of different ions (e.g., V2+ and V3+). The 2+ ion will be formed from vanadium metal by oxidizing the metal and removing two electrons:

V ⟶ V2+ + 2e

Removing the another electron gives

V2+ ⟶ V3+ + 3e

Each time the vanadium is oxidized (and loses another electron), its oxidation state increases by 1. If the process is reversed, or electrons are added, the oxidation state decreases.

Rules to Determine Oxidation States

  • An atom of a free element has an oxidation number of 0. For example, Cl atom in Cl2 has an oxidation number of 0, and the same is true for H atom in H2.

  • A monatomic ion has an oxidation number equal to its charge. For example, the oxidation number of Cu2+ is +2, Br is 1, and so on.

  • In compounds, alkali metals (Group 1A) always have an oxidation number of +1, while alkaline earth metals (Group 2A) always have an oxidation number of +2.

  • Fluorine has an oxidation number of 1.

  • Hydrogen has an oxidation number of +1 in the compounds of nonmetals such as CH4, NH3, H2O, HCl, and so on. While in the compounds of metals it has an oxidation number of 1 such as LiH, NaH, CaH2, LiAlH4, and so on.

  • Oxygen usually has an oxidation number of 2. Exceptions occur for molecules and polyatomic ions that contain O-O bonds (peroxide), such as O3, H2O2, and the O22 ion, where the oxygen has an oxidation number of 1. Oxygen in F2O has an oxidation state of +2.

  • The elements in Group VIIA (Halogen) have an oxidation number of 1 in compounds, unless combined with oxygen or fluorine. For example, the oxidation number of Cl in the ion ClO4 is +7 since O has an oxidation number of -2 and the overall charge on the ion is -1.

  • The elements in Group VIIA (Halogen) have an oxidation number of 1 in compounds, unless combined with oxygen or fluorine. For example, the oxidation number of Cl in the ion ClO4 is +7 since O has an oxidation number of -2 and the overall charge on the ion is -1.

  • The sum of the oxidation numbers for all atoms in a neutral compound is equal to zero, while the sum for all atoms in a polyatomic ion is equal to the charge on the ion. For example, consider the polyatomic ion NO3 in which the O atoms have a total oxidation number of 23=6. Since the overall charge on the ion is -1, then the oxidation number of N must be +5.

BALANCING REDOX REACTION

Balancing redox reactions is slightly more complex than balancing standard reactions, but still follows a relatively simple set of rules. One major difference is the necessity to know the half-reactions of the involved reactants.


Half-reactions are often useful in that two half reactions can be added to get a total net equation. Although the half-reactions must be known to complete a redox reaction, it is often possible to figure them out without having to use a half-reaction table.


It is demonstrated in the acidic and basic solution examples. Besides the general rules for neutral conditions, additional rules must be applied for aqueous reactions in acidic or basic conditions.

To balance any redox reaction in neutral is to separate the reaction into half-reactions. The substance being reduced will have electrons as reactants, and the oxidized substance will have electrons as products.


For example, the reaction between Fe and Cu :

\( \text{Cu}^{+}(aq) + \text{Fe}(s) \rightarrow \text{Fe}^{3+}(aq) + \text{Cu}(s) \)

To balance the reaction:


1. Separate the half-reactions. By searching for the reduction potential, one can find two separate reactions:
\( \text{Cu}^{+}(aq) + e^- \rightarrow \text{Cu}(s) \quad \text{and} \quad \text{Fe}^{3+}(aq) + e^- \rightarrow \text{Fe}(s) \)

2. The copper reaction has a higher potential and thus is being reduced. Iron is being oxidized so the half-reaction should be flipped.
\( 2\text{Fe}(s) \longrightarrow \text{Fe}^{3+}(aq) + e^- \)


3. Balance the electrons in the equations. In this case, the electrons are simply balanced by multiplying the entire copper half-reaction by 3 and leave the other half.
\( 3\text{Cu}^{+}(aq) + 3e^- \longrightarrow 3\text{Cu}(s) \quad \text{and} \quad \text{Fe}^{3+}(aq) + e^- \longrightarrow \text{Fe}(s) \)


4. Adding the equations give: \( 3\text{Cu}^{+}(aq) + 3e^- + \text{Fe}(s) \longrightarrow 3\text{Cu}(s) + \text{Fe}^{3+}(aq) + 3e^- \)
\( {3Cu}^+(aq) + Fe(s) \longrightarrow {3Fe}^{3+}(aq) + Cu(s) \)

Each reaction is balanced by adjusting coefficients and adding H2O, H+, and e- in this order:


  1. Balance elements in the equation other than O and H
  2. Balance the oxygen atoms by adding the appropriate number of water molecules to the opposite side of the equation.
  3. Balance the hydrogen atoms (including those added in step 2 to balance the oxygen atom) by adding H+ ions to the opposite side of the equation.
  4. Add up the charges on each side. Make them equal by adding enough electrons (e-) to the more positive side. (Tips: H+ and e- are always on the same side)
  5. The e- on each side must be made equal; if they are not equal, they must be multiplied by appropriate integers (the lowest common multiple) to be made the same.
  6. The half-equations are added together, canceling out the electrons to form one balanced equation. Common terms should also be canceled out.

Examples for Acidic Reaction:

\( {{Cr}_2O}_7^{2-}(aq) + {HNO}_2(aq) \longrightarrow {Cr}^{3+}(aq) + {NO}_3^-(aq) \)

To balance the reaction:


1. Separate the half-reactions.
\( {{Cr}_2O}_7^{2-}(aq) \longrightarrow {Cr}^{3+}(aq) \) -&- \( {HNO}_2(aq) \longrightarrow {NO}_3^-(aq) \)


2. Balance elements other than O and H. In this example, only chromium needs to be balanced.
\( {{Cr}_2O}_7^{2-}(aq) \longrightarrow {2Cr}^{3+}(aq) \)


3. Add H2O to balance oxygen. The chromium reaction needs to be balanced by adding 7 H2O molecules. The other reaction also needs to be balanced by adding one water molecule.
\begin{align*} (1) & \quad -\ {{Cr}_2O}_7^{2-}(aq) & \longrightarrow & \ {2Cr}^{3+}(aq) + 7H_2O(l) \\ (2) & \quad -\ {HNO}_2(aq) + H_2O(l) & \longrightarrow & \ {NO}_3^-(aq) \end{align*}

4. Balance hydrogen by adding protons (H^+) . 14 protons need to be added to the left side of the chromium reaction to balance the 14 (2 per water molecule * 7 water molecules) hydrogens. 3 protons need to be added to the right side of the other reaction.
\begin{align*} (1) & \quad -\ {{14H^+ + Cr}_2O}_7^{2-}(aq) & \longrightarrow & \ {2Cr}^{3+}(aq) + 7H_2O(l) \\ (2) & \quad -\ {HNO}_2(aq) + H_2O(l) & \longrightarrow & \ 3H^+ + {NO}_3^-(aq) \end{align*}

5. Balance the charge of each equation with electrons. The chromium reaction has (14+) + (2-) = 12+ on the left side and (2 * 3+) = 6+ on the right side. To balance, add 6 electrons (each with a charge of -1) to the left side:
\( 6e^- + {{14H^+ + Cr}_2O}_7^{2-}(aq) \longrightarrow {2Cr}^{3+}(aq) + 7H_2O(l) \)

For the other reaction, there is no charge on the left and a (3+) + (-1) = 2+ charge on the right. So, add 2 electrons to the right side:
\( {HNO}_2(aq) + H_2O(l) \longrightarrow 3H^+ + {NO}_3^- (aq) + 2e^- \)


6. Scale the reactions so that the electrons are equal. The chromium reaction has 6e- and the other reaction has 2e-, so it should be multiplied by 3. This gives:
\( \therefore 3{HNO}_2(aq) + {3H}_2O(l) \longrightarrow 9H^+ + {3NO}_3^-(aq) + 6e^- \)

7. Add the reactions and cancel out common terms.
\begin{align*} 3{HNO}_2(aq) + {3H}_2O(l) & \longrightarrow 9H^+ + {3NO}_3^-(aq) + 6e^- \\ 6e^- + {{14H^+ + Cr}_2O}_7^{2-}(aq) & \longrightarrow {2Cr}^{3+}(aq) + 7H_2O(l) \end{align*}

\( 3{HNO}_2(aq) + {3H}_2O(l) + 6e^- + {{14H^+ + Cr}_2O}_7^{2-}(aq) \) \( \longrightarrow 9H^+ + {3NO}_3^-(aq) + 6e^- + {2Cr}^{3+}(aq) + 7H_2O(l) \)
\( \therefore 3{HNO}_2(aq) + {{5H^+ + Cr}_2O}_7^{2-}(aq) \longrightarrow {3NO}_3^-(aq) + {2Cr}^{3+}(aq) + 4H_2O(l) \)

If the reaction is being balanced in a basic solution, the acidic steps are modified with the addition of one step between #3 and #4:


3. Balance the hydrogen atoms (including those added in step 2 to balance the oxygen atom) by adding H+ ions to the opposite side of the equation.

(3b) Add the appropriate number of OH- to neutralize H+ and to convert into water molecules.

4. Add up the charges on each side. Make them equal by adding enough electrons (e-) to the more positive side. (Tips: H+ and e- are always on the same side)


Examples for Basic Reaction:


\( \text{Ag}(s) + {Zn}^{2+}(aq) \longrightarrow {Ag}_2O(aq) + \text{Zn}(s) \)

1. Separate per half-reaction.
\( \text{Ag}(s) \longrightarrow {Ag}_2O(aq) \quad \text{and} \quad {Zn}^{2+}(aq) \longrightarrow \text{Zn}(s) \)


2. Balance elements other than O and H.
\begin{align*} 2\text{Ag}(s) & \longrightarrow {Ag}_2O(aq) \\ {Zn}^{2+}(aq) & \longrightarrow \text{Zn}(s) \end{align*}


3. Add H2O to balance oxygen.
\begin{align*} H_2O(l) + 2\text{Ag}(s) & \longrightarrow {Ag}_2O(aq) \\ {Zn}^{2+}(aq) & \longrightarrow \text{Zn}(s) \end{align*}

4. Balance hydrogen with protons.
\begin{align*} H_2O(l) + 2Ag(s) & \longrightarrow {Ag}_2O(aq) + {2H}^+(aq) \\ {Zn}^{2+}(aq) & \longrightarrow Zn(s) \end{align*}


5. Balance the charge with e-.
\begin{align*} H_2O(l) + 2Ag(s) & \longrightarrow {Ag}_2O(aq) + {2H}^+(aq) + {2e}^- \\ {Zn}^{2+}(aq) + {2e}^- & \longrightarrow Zn(s) \end{align*}


6. Scale the reactions so that they have an equal number of electrons. In this case, it is already done.

7. Combine the reactions and cancel the electrons.
\( H_2O(l) + 2Ag(s) + {Zn}^{2+} + {2e}^-(aq) \longrightarrow Zn(s) + {Ag}_2O(aq) + {2H}^+(aq) + {2e}^- \)

\( H_2O(l) + 2Ag(s) + {Zn}^{2+}(aq) \longrightarrow Zn(s) + {Ag}_2O(aq) + {2H}^+(aq) \)


8. Add OH- to balance H+. There are 2 net protons in this equation, so add 2 OH- ions to each side.
\( H_2O(l) + 2Ag(s) + {Zn}^{2+}(aq) + {2OH}^-(aq) \longrightarrow Zn(s) + {Ag}_2O(aq) + {2H}^+(aq) + {2OH}^-(aq) \)


9. Combine OH- ions and H+ ions that are present on the same side to form water and cancel common terms.
\( H_2O(l) + 2Ag(s) + {Zn}^{2+}(aq) + {2OH}^-(aq) \longrightarrow Zn(s) + {Ag}_2O(aq) + {2H}_2O(l) \) \( \therefore 2Ag(s) + {Zn}^{2+}(aq) + {2OH}^-(aq) \longrightarrow Zn(s) + {Ag}_2O(aq) \)

Combination reactions are among the simplest redox reactions and, as the name suggests, involves "combining" elements to form a chemical compound. As usual, oxidation and reduction occur together.


The general equation for a combination reaction is: A + B AB


Ex: 2H2 + O2 2H2O(l)

decomposition reaction is the reverse of a combination reaction, the breakdown of a chemical compound into individual elements: AB A + B


Ex: 2H2O 2H2 + O2


There are some exceptions in Decomposition such as the autoionization of water. It does not involve a transfer of electrons between species. Instead, it is a purely acid-base equilibrium reaction where water molecules act as both acids and bases.


2H2O(l) H3O+(aq) + OH-(aq)

A single replacement reaction involves the "replacing" of an element in the reactants with another element in the products: A + BC AB + C.


Ex: Cl2 + NaBr NaCl + Br2

A double replacement reaction is similar to a single replacement reaction, but involves "replacing" two elements in the reactants, with two in the products: + CD AD + CBAB.


Ex: gSO4(aq) + Na2C2O4(aq) MgC2O4(s) + Na2SO4(aq)

In disproportionation reactions, a single substance can be both oxidized and reduced. These are known as disproportionation reactions, with the following general equation:


2A A+n + A-n

The common example of this reaction is the decomposition of Hydrogen Peroxide:


2H2O2 2H2O + O2

In this reaction, hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) decomposes into water (H2O) and oxygen (O2) gas. The oxygen in hydrogen peroxide (-1 oxidation state) is simultaneously oxidized to 0 in O2 and reduced to -2 in H2O. Therefore, hydrogen peroxide is both oxidized and reduced simultaneously.

REDOX APPLICATIONS
Redox reactions have numerous applications in various industries.

They are used in the production of chemicals, such as caustic soda, chlorine, and nitric acid, which are essential components in many products like fertilizers and cleaning agents.


Redox reactions are also used in the electroplating process, where a thin layer of material is applied to an object, such as gold-plated jewelry.


In the field of metallurgy, redox reactions are used for extracting metals from ores and combustion of fuel. They are also used in the treatment of wastewater, where redox reactions are employed to convert pollutants into harmless molecules.

Redox reactions are also used in the production of fuels, such as ethanol, where they are used to convert simple molecules into more complex ones that can be used as fuel. In the pharmaceutical industry, redox reactions are used for the determination of valganciclovir hydrochloride (VLGH) in pure samples.


In the field of energy production, batteries, and electrochemical cells use redox reactions to produce electrical energy. These cells are used in vehicles to supply all the electrical needs of the vehicles.


Overall, redox reactions play a crucial role in various industries, from the production of chemicals and fuels to the treatment of wastewater and the production of energy.

ELECTROCHEMICAL CELL

An electrochemical cell is a device that generates electrical energy from chemical reactions or uses electrical energy to cause chemical reactions to occur. It consists of two half-cells, each containing an electrode and an electrolyte. The electrodes are connected by a salt bridge or a porous membrane, which allows for ionic contact between the two half-cells without allowing them to mix and react with each other.

An electrochemical cell splits the oxidant and reductant in a manner that allows electrons to flow through an external circuit from the reductant (which gets oxidized) to the oxidant (which causes reduction) while preventing them from physically touching each other.

ELECTROCHEMICAL CELL COMPONENTS

Electrochemical Cell Diagram

The main components of an electrochemical cell are:


ANODE

The electrode where oxidation occur. This is always the case, but the sign of the anode switches between galvanic and electrolytic modes

In a galvanic cell this is the negative electrode, from the perspective of the external circuit, where the negative electrons flow to the positive terminal, which is the other electrode, making the anode the negative electrode.

Meanwhile in the electrolytic cell, its the positive electrode. The electrode sign is not being determined by the cell reaction, but by the external power supply that is driving the reaction in the nonspontaneous direction.

CATHODE

The electrode where Reduction occurs at the cathode. The cathode has the opposite signs of the anode, so it is [+] in a galvanic/voltaic cell, and [-] in an electrolytic cell.


SALT BRIDGE

The salt bridge has two basic functions. First it completes the circuit and prevents charge buildup, which would stop the flow of electrons. The salt bridge prevents the buildup of negative charge by enabling an internal circuit that allows ions to complete the circuit and prevent charge buildup at the electrode.

EXTERNAL CIRCUIT/WIRE

The external circuit allows electrons to flow from the anode to the cathode. In the case of a galvanic cell, this is a spontaneous flow and can be used to provide energy that can produce heat or do work. In the case of an electrolytic cell this requires an energy source to make it work.

ELECTROCHEMICAL CELL WORKING PRINCIPLE

Electrochemical cell works on metal-single-displacement reaction which involves metallic material such like zinc and copper. The reaction would be written like these:


\( \text{Zn}(s) + \text{Cu}^{2+}(aq) \longrightarrow \text{Cu}(s) + \text{Zn}^{2+}(aq) \)

or

\( \text{Cu}(s) + \text{Zn}^{2+}(s) \longrightarrow \text{Zn}(s) + \text{Cu}^{2+}(s) \)

To determine whether the reaction is spontaneous or nonspontaneous, Standard Reduction Potential would be use to see each elements potential and calculating it using formula:


\( E_{\text{Cell}}^ = E_{\text{Cathode}}^ - E_{\text{Anode}}^ \)

If \( E_{\text{Cell}}^ > 0 \) , the reaction is spontaneous in the direction written. If \( E_{\text{Cell}}^ < 0 \), the reaction is non-spontaneous in the direction written. In this case, the reverse reaction is spontaneous, and the cell potential would be the negative of the calculated value.



By looking on the Standard Potential Table, Zinc half-reaction would produce -0.76 V and Copper would produce +0.34 V, and the Potential Cell equation will be: 0.34 (-0.76) = 1.2V.


Since zinc is more active than copper, meaning metallic zinc will spontaneously dissolve in a solution with Copper (II) ions. This means the top reaction above has a negative free energy (it is spontaneous) and could be used to do work, and the bottom reaction above has a positive free energy, which means nothing would happen, unless we added an external source of energy to drive it.

There is a shorthand notation used to specify the conditions of an electrochemical cell. The notation shows the anode on the left and cathode on the right. The concentrations of important species in each of the half reactions are included. Spectator ions are not included in the notation. Phase boundaries are shown with a single line (|) and a salt bridge with a double line (||).

Electrochemical cells are used as a battery from portable electronics like cellphones and digital cameras to larger systems like electric vehicles and renewable energy storage systems.
